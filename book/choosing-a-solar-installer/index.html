Choosing a Solar Installer

What You Will Learn

Section titled “What You Will Learn”
  • Compare installers using the same criteria.
  • Read quote assumptions instead of focusing only on headline price.
  • Identify contract and communication details before signing.
  • Recognize sales pressure, vague scope, and quote gaps before they become contract problems.

Real-World Scenarios

Section titled “Real-World Scenarios”
Scenario 1: one installer is much cheaper than the others.

The price may be better, or it may exclude electrical work, roof work, monitoring, permits, warranties, or realistic production inputs. Compare scope line by line before calling it the better deal.

Scenario 2: the salesperson promises savings that are not in the proposal.

Verbal promises are hard to enforce. Ask for production inputs, utility rules, equipment details, exclusions, cancellation terms, and financing terms in writing.

Scenario 3: the company selling the project will not install it.

Subcontracting is not bad by default, but accountability must be clear. Know who designs, permits, installs, inspects, supports, and services the system.

Key Ideas

Section titled “Key Ideas”

A Quote Is a Design Proposal, Not Just a Price

Section titled “A Quote Is a Design Proposal, Not Just a Price”

System size, equipment, production estimate, roof layout, electrical work, financing, warranty terms, and schedule all shape the value of the proposal.

Installer Accountability Should Be Clear

Section titled “Installer Accountability Should Be Clear”

Homeowners should know who sells the project, who designs it, who installs it, who handles permits, and who provides post-install support.

Contract Terms Matter Before Work Starts

Section titled “Contract Terms Matter Before Work Starts”

Review payment milestones, cancellation terms, change orders, lien releases, equipment substitutions, and production estimate language before signing.

Practical Steps

Section titled “Practical Steps”
  1. Ask each installer to explain system size, estimated production, and assumptions in plain language.
  2. Compare warranties, payment terms, cancellation terms, and who performs the installation.
  3. Avoid treating estimated savings as a guarantee.
  4. Ask for model numbers, roof layout, cash price, financed price, and exclusions from each installer.
  5. Keep a written record of promises, changes, and open questions before signing.

Questions To Ask

Section titled “Questions To Ask”
  • Who will perform the installation: employees, subcontractors, or a partner crew?
  • What assumptions drive the production and savings estimate?
  • What contract terms apply if equipment, design, or timeline changes?
  • What is excluded from this quote?
  • What happens if the site survey changes the design or production estimate?
  • Who is my point of contact after signing, during installation, and after permission to operate?
  • Can I review the contract, financing documents, and cancellation terms before a signing appointment?

Watch-Outs

Section titled “Watch-Outs”
  • Choosing only by lowest monthly payment or highest claimed savings.
  • Signing before the roof, panel, and utility requirements are understood.
  • Accepting verbal promises that do not appear in the proposal or contract.
  • Being rushed through a contract or financing application without time to read it.
  • Comparing a lease, loan, and cash purchase as if ownership and obligations are identical.
  • Ignoring complaints, licensing issues, or unclear subcontractor relationships.
