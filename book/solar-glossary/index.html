Solar Glossary

Use this glossary when a quote, contract, installer, utility, or permit document uses a term that sounds more technical than it needs to be.

Solar Equipment

Section titled “Solar Equipment”

Solar panel
A framed module made of solar cells that turns sunlight into direct current electricity. Think of each panel as one tile in a larger roof-mounted power source.

Solar cell
The small electrical unit inside a panel that reacts to sunlight. A panel contains many cells wired together.

PV
Short for photovoltaic, which means turning light into electricity. If a document says PV system, it usually means the solar panel system.

Array
A group of solar panels connected as one system. If panels are the individual tiles, the array is the whole tiled section.

Module
Another word for a solar panel. Installers and equipment sheets often use module because it is the industry term.

Inverter
Equipment that converts direct current from the panels into alternating current used by the home. It is like a translator between the panels and your house wiring.

String inverter
One central inverter connected to groups of panels. It can be efficient and cost-effective, but shading on one part of a string can affect more than one panel.

Microinverter
A small inverter attached to each panel. It lets each panel convert power separately, which can help when roof planes or shade conditions vary.

Power optimizer
Panel-level electronics used with some string inverter systems to manage each panel’s output before power reaches the inverter.

Rapid shutdown
A safety feature that lets firefighters or workers quickly reduce voltage from roof equipment during an emergency.

Racking
The rails, mounts, clamps, and hardware that attach panels to the roof or ground. Racking is the frame that holds the array in place.

Flashing
Waterproofing material installed around roof attachments. Its job is similar to the flashing around a chimney or vent: keep water out where something passes through the roof.

Conduit
Protective tubing that carries electrical wiring between solar equipment, the inverter, meter, and electrical panel.

Junction box
An enclosure where wires connect safely. It keeps electrical connections protected and accessible.

Combiner box
A box that combines multiple solar circuits before sending power onward. Think of it as a traffic merge for solar wiring.

Disconnect switch
A switch that lets solar equipment be shut off for service or safety.

Monitoring system
Software or hardware that shows whether the system is producing power. Monitoring is like a dashboard, not a guarantee that every billing issue is solved.

Electricity Basics

Section titled “Electricity Basics”

Watt
A unit of power. It describes how fast electricity is being produced or used at a moment in time.

Kilowatt
One thousand watts. Solar system size is often shown in kilowatts.

Kilowatt-hour
A unit of energy over time. Your electric bill usually measures usage in kilowatt-hours. If watts are speed, kilowatt-hours are distance traveled.

DC
Direct current electricity. Solar panels produce DC electricity.

AC
Alternating current electricity. Homes and the electric grid generally use AC electricity.

Voltage
Electrical pressure that pushes current through wires. Higher voltage needs proper equipment and safety handling.

Current
The flow of electricity, measured in amps. If voltage is pressure, current is the amount flowing.

Amp
A unit of electrical current. Main electrical panels are often described by amperage, such as 100 amp or 200 amp service.

Circuit
A controlled path for electricity. Solar systems, appliances, outlets, and backup loads all use circuits.

Breaker
A safety switch in an electrical panel that shuts off a circuit when current is too high.

Main service panel
The home’s primary breaker panel. Solar projects often require checking whether this panel has enough capacity for the proposed system.

Subpanel
A smaller panel fed from the main panel. Battery backup systems may use a critical loads subpanel.

Load
Anything that uses electricity, such as lights, appliances, outlets, or HVAC equipment.

Critical load
An electrical load you want backed up during an outage, such as a refrigerator, medical device, internet equipment, or selected lights.

Grounding
Safety wiring that helps direct fault current away from people and equipment.

Bonding
Connecting metal parts together so they share a safe electrical path.

System Size and Performance

Section titled “System Size and Performance”

System size
The rated size of the solar system, usually shown in kW DC. It is useful for comparing quotes, but it does not by itself tell you annual savings.

Nameplate rating
The lab-rated capacity of equipment, such as a 400 watt panel. Real-world production varies with heat, shade, weather, roof angle, and equipment losses.

Production estimate
An estimate of how much electricity the system will produce, usually over one year. This should be treated as a model based on assumptions.

Annual production
The estimated or measured electricity a solar system produces in a year, usually in kWh.

Capacity factor
How much energy a system produces compared with running at full rated power all the time. Solar has daylight and weather limits, so this is far below 100%.

Clipping
When panels could produce more power than the inverter can convert at that moment. Some clipping can be normal if the system was designed that way.

Degradation
The gradual decline in panel output over time. Panels usually still work for decades, but output slowly drops.

Shade analysis
An assessment of how trees, chimneys, vents, roof shapes, or nearby buildings affect sunlight.

Azimuth
The compass direction a roof plane faces. South, east, and west orientations can produce different patterns of solar energy.

Tilt
The angle of the panels or roof. Tilt affects how sunlight hits the panels through the year.

Irradiance
The amount of solar energy hitting a surface. Think of it as sunlight strength on the roof.

Solar access
How much usable sunlight a location receives after shade and orientation are considered.

Setback
Required clear space on the roof for fire access, code compliance, or maintenance. Setbacks can reduce how many panels fit.

Roof and Property Terms

Section titled “Roof and Property Terms”

Roof plane
One flat section of a roof. A home may have several roof planes facing different directions.

Roof penetration
A place where mounting hardware passes through the roofing surface. Good flashing and workmanship matter here.

Structural review
A check that the roof structure can support the added weight and wind forces of the solar system.

Rafter
A structural roof member beneath the roof surface. Mounts are often attached into rafters or other approved structural members.

Truss
A pre-built roof support structure. Truss roofs may need specific attachment methods or engineering review.

Roof age
How old the roof is. If the roof may need replacement soon, installing solar first can create future removal and reinstallation costs.

Main panel upgrade
Replacing or upgrading the home’s electrical panel or service equipment to support solar or battery equipment.

Meter location
Where the utility meter is installed. It can affect conduit routing, interconnection work, and equipment placement.

HOA approval
Approval from a homeowners association. Some areas limit how much an HOA can restrict solar, but paperwork may still be required.

Permit
Local government approval to install the system. Permits help verify code compliance before and after installation.

Inspection
A review by a local inspector, utility, or authority to confirm the installation meets requirements.

Utility and Billing Terms

Section titled “Utility and Billing Terms”

Grid
The utility electric network. Most home solar systems remain connected to the grid.

Interconnection
The process of getting permission to connect the solar system to the utility grid.

Permission to operate
Utility approval to turn on the system for normal operation. Installation being complete does not always mean the system can operate yet.

Net metering
A billing structure that credits exported solar energy against electricity you use from the grid. Rules vary by utility and location.

Export credit
The value the utility gives for excess solar energy sent to the grid.

Retail rate
The price you pay the utility for electricity. Some savings estimates assume exported solar is worth this amount, but that depends on local rules.

Avoided cost
A lower credit rate sometimes used for exported energy. It is usually closer to what the utility says it avoids paying for generation.

Time-of-use rate
A rate plan where electricity prices change by time of day. Solar and battery value can change a lot under this structure.

Demand charge
A charge based on the highest power draw during a billing period. This is common for some commercial bills and less common for homes, but homeowners should know if it appears.

Fixed charge
A monthly utility charge that usually remains even if solar reduces energy usage.

Minimum bill
The lowest bill amount a utility may charge even when net usage is low.

True-up
A periodic utility billing reconciliation, often annual, that settles credits and charges.

Bill credit
A credit applied to the utility bill for exported solar energy or other program rules.

Rate plan
The utility pricing structure assigned to your account. Solar savings depend heavily on the actual rate plan.

Battery and Backup Terms

Section titled “Battery and Backup Terms”

Battery
Equipment that stores electricity for later use. A battery can support backup power, time-of-use shifting, or both depending on design and rules.

Usable capacity
The amount of stored energy you can actually use, usually measured in kWh. It may be lower than the battery’s total nameplate capacity.

Power rating
How much power a battery can deliver at one time. Capacity is how much fuel is in the tank; power rating is how fast it can come out.

Backup duration
How long a battery can support selected loads. This depends on battery size and what you run.

Critical loads panel
A subpanel containing only the circuits you want backed up. It helps avoid trying to power the whole house from a limited battery.

Whole-home backup
A system designed to back up most or all home circuits. It may require more battery capacity, load management, and electrical work.

Load management
Equipment or controls that decide which circuits can run during backup. It helps prevent too many devices from drawing power at once.

State of charge
How full the battery is, usually shown as a percentage.

Round-trip efficiency
How much energy remains after charging and discharging the battery. Some energy is lost in the process.

Island mode
Operation where the home is separated from the grid during an outage and powered by solar, battery, or both.

Transfer switch
Equipment that safely switches selected loads between grid power and backup power.

Quote, Contract, and Finance Terms

Section titled “Quote, Contract, and Finance Terms”

Gross cost
The full contract price before incentives, tax credits, or rebates.

Net cost
The cost after estimated incentives. Treat this carefully because not every homeowner can use every incentive.

Cost per watt
Gross system cost divided by system watts. It is a useful comparison metric, but only when scope and equipment are comparable.

Cash price
The price if you pay without installer-arranged financing. Ask for this even if you plan to finance.

Dealer fee
A financing-related cost that can raise the financed price. A low APR loan may still be expensive if the dealer fee is large.

APR
Annual percentage rate for a loan. It is important, but it does not tell the full story without principal, fees, term, and payment structure.

Loan term
How long the loan lasts. Longer terms can lower monthly payment while increasing total payments.

Lease
An arrangement where another company owns the system and you pay to use it.

PPA
Power purchase agreement. You buy the electricity produced by a system owned by another company, usually at a set price per kWh.

Escalator
A scheduled annual increase in lease or PPA payments. Even a small percentage can matter over a long contract.

Tax credit
A credit that may reduce tax owed if you qualify. It is not the same as an instant discount or guaranteed refund.

Rebate
Money back from a utility, government, manufacturer, or program. Rebates usually have eligibility rules and funding limits.

REC
Renewable energy certificate. It represents the renewable attribute of generated electricity. Ownership may matter in some incentive programs.

SREC
Solar renewable energy certificate. A solar-specific REC used in some markets.

Production guarantee
A promise about system production, if included. Read the details because remedies and exclusions vary.

Workmanship warranty
Installer coverage for installation labor and workmanship. This is separate from manufacturer equipment warranties.

Manufacturer warranty
Coverage from the company that made the panel, inverter, battery, or other equipment.

Performance warranty
A manufacturer promise about how much panel output should remain after a period of years.

Change order
A contract change after signing, often because site conditions or scope changed.

Lien
A legal claim related to unpaid work or materials. Homeowners should understand contract language about lien releases and payment responsibility.

Cancellation window
The period when you can cancel after signing, if applicable. The method and deadline matter.

Installation Process Terms

Section titled “Installation Process Terms”

Site survey
A detailed review of the property before final design. This may include roof, attic, electrical panel, meter, and shade checks.

Design plan
The proposed layout, equipment, electrical details, and construction plan for the system.

Plan set
Technical drawings submitted for permit approval. It is the installation blueprint.

Engineering stamp
Approval from a licensed engineer when required by local rules or project conditions.

Procurement
Ordering and securing equipment for the job.

Installation day
The day crews mount equipment and complete much of the physical work. Final approval can still take longer.

Commissioning
Testing and setting up the system after installation so it can operate correctly.

PTO
Short for permission to operate. This is utility approval for normal operation.

As-built documents
Final documents showing what was actually installed.

Monitoring app
The app or web portal used to view system status and production.

Red-Flag Phrases to Clarify

Section titled “Red-Flag Phrases to Clarify”

No electric bill
Usually means estimated bill offset, not that every utility charge disappears.

Free solar
Usually means financed, leased, subsidized, or no upfront payment. Ask who owns the system and what you pay over time.

Battery ready
May mean only that a future battery is possible, not that battery equipment or wiring is included.

Backup power included
Ask which circuits are backed up, for how long, and whether extra panel work is included.

Tier 1 panels
A broad industry label that does not replace exact model numbers, warranty terms, and design details.

Estimated savings
A projection based on assumptions. Ask for the utility rate, production estimate, usage data, and escalation assumptions behind it.
