Understand Your Utility Bill

What You Will Learn

Section titled “What You Will Learn”
  • Find the bill fields installers need for early sizing.
  • Understand why kilowatt-hours matter more than the bill total.
  • Recognize rate-plan details that can affect solar value.
  • Use bill details to challenge savings claims before accepting a proposal.

Real-World Scenarios

Section titled “Real-World Scenarios”
Scenario 1: one summer bill is unusually high.

A system sized from one high bill can be too large. Use 12 months of kWh usage, especially when air conditioning, guests, pool equipment, or temporary work-from-home schedules caused a spike.

Scenario 2: the bill has fixed charges that solar cannot erase.

Solar production can reduce energy charges, but connection fees, minimum bills, taxes, program charges, and some demand or delivery charges may remain. A credible savings estimate should show what charges continue after installation.

Scenario 3: the household plans to add an EV next year.

Past usage can understate future needs. Tell installers about new loads so the design includes future use, not only old bills.

Key Ideas

Section titled “Key Ideas”

Usage Is Measured in Kilowatt-Hours

Section titled “Usage Is Measured in Kilowatt-Hours”

The kWh total shows how much electricity the home used during the billing period. Dollar totals also include rates, fees, taxes, and other charges.

Seasonal Usage Changes the Design Discussion

Section titled “Seasonal Usage Changes the Design Discussion”

Air conditioning, heat pumps, pool pumps, EV charging, and household schedules can make summer and winter usage look very different.

Rate Plans Shape Bill Impact

Section titled “Rate Plans Shape Bill Impact”

Time-of-use periods, fixed charges, minimum bills, and export credit rules can affect how solar production translates into bill savings.

Practical Steps

Section titled “Practical Steps”
  1. Find your monthly kilowatt-hour usage, not just the total dollar amount.
  2. Check whether the bill mentions time-of-use rates, demand charges, or net metering.
  3. Compare summer and winter usage if you have several bills available.
  4. Identify fixed monthly charges, minimum bills, taxes, and fees that may remain after solar.
  5. Write down planned changes such as EV charging, heat pumps, induction cooking, pool equipment, or added household members.

Bill Reading Worksheet

Section titled “Bill Reading Worksheet”
Bill itemWhat to findHow to use it
Monthly kWhEnergy used during the billing period.Ask whether the quote used one bill, 12 months, or an estimate.
Rate planFlat rate, time-of-use periods, tiered rates, or other structure.Ask how production timing affects bill savings.
Fixed chargesCharges that appear even when usage is low.Ask which charges remain after solar.
Export credit rulesHow the utility credits excess solar sent to the grid.Ask whether exported energy is valued at retail rate, avoided cost, or another rate.
Seasonal patternMonths with unusually high or low usage.Ask whether system size is based on normal usage or a temporary spike.

Questions To Ask

Section titled “Questions To Ask”
  • Which bill fields did you use to estimate system size?
  • How do time-of-use rates change the value of afternoon or evening energy?
  • What bill charges may remain even after solar is installed?
  • What annual kWh usage did you assume?
  • What export credit or net billing rule did you model?
  • How would the design change if I add an EV, heat pump, or battery later?

What Good Looks Like

Section titled “What Good Looks Like”

Watch-Outs

Section titled “Watch-Outs”
  • Sizing a system from one unusually high or low bill.
  • Ignoring planned changes like an EV, heat pump, or added household member.
  • Assuming every charge on the bill can be eliminated by solar production.
  • Comparing savings estimates that use different utility escalation assumptions.
  • Letting an installer use a bill total when the kWh and rate-plan details are available.

Next Action

Section titled “Next Action”

Save a PDF or photo of a recent electric bill, then log into your utility account and download 12 months of kWh usage if available.
